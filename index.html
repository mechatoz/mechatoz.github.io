Compressed Sensing and Parallel Acquisition: Theory and Application

Parallel acquisition systems are employed successfully in a variety of different sensing applications when a single sensor cannot provide enough measurements for a high-quality reconstruction. Compressed sensing (CS), random sub-sampling theory dependent on the sparsity of signal, has been used to establish the theoretical improvements of such systems by providing recovery guarantees for which, subject to appropriate conditions, the number of measurements required per sensor decreases linearly with the total number of sensors. My research objective is to improve such guarantees in the parallel acquisition system, develope CS-based imaging fundamentals, and apply them in practical applications, e.g. parallel MRI and light-field imaging.

Convolutional Dictionary Learning: Acceleration and Convergence

The so-called convolutional dictionary learning (or sparse coding) is essential in variant convolutional neural network models. An accelerated algorithm development is a fundamental issue in learning convolutional dictionary from "big data". In addition, the convergence guarantee is another crucial issue in learning convolutional dictionary, particularly when the learned kernels are applied to solve some inverse problems. While establishing the accelerated and stable algorithm in convolutional dictionary learning, I am actively applying them to signal recovery with higher accuracy in medical imaging and computational photography.

Adaptive Signal Processing in Computational Imaging

Most imaging devices are suffered from various types of noise relying on their own imaging physics; in particular for the highly under-sampling scanners, severe residual aliasing artifacts and amplified noise can easily occur. The optimal adaptive control in imaging devices can provide an alternative solution to recover high-quality images, while not harming the image recovery stability. Based on stochastic modeling, my aim is to improve SNR or reduce MSE in reconstructed images by exploiting adaptive signal processing techniques to different imaging techniques.

Image Analysis in Neuroimaging

Current technologies in automatic medical image analysis, e.g., registration, segmentation, and classification, are imperfect in their accuracy. A combination of multiple information from intensity and gradient would be useful to improve the performance, e.g., in multi-modal image registration.

In neuroscience society, an appropriate statistical image analysis framework is largely absent, e.g., for hypothesis testing. A combination of complete and incomplete paired data results in larger pool size; it is expected to increase detection power. The stronger hypothesis testing method is particularly useful to reliably detect subtle changes in athletes who experienced subconcussive head blows.
